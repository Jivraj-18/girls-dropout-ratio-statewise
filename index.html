Who improved most, who barely moved?

The spread in improvement speed is as important as the spread in current rates. Some states dropped 20+ points. Others stayed essentially flat.

Improvement speed tiers (2018-19 to 2024-25):
  • Sharp improvers (+15pp): Bihar (-22.7), Jharkhand (-20.0), Tripura (-19.5), Arunachal Pradesh (-17.3), Assam (-15.4)
  • Steady improvers (3-15pp): 18 states making consistent progress
  • Flat or worsening (<3pp): 11 states including Mizoram (+5.9, worse) and Odisha (+5.4, worse)

Stability vs. volatility: What year-to-year swings signal

Not all improvement is the same. Some states improve steadily, year after year. Others jump, fall, jump again. The difference matters: steady improvement suggests systemic change; volatility suggests external shocks or measurement inconsistency.

Stable improvers (consistent year-over-year declines):
  • Jharkhand: -20.0pp over 7 years (2.9pp/year avg) — Steady, predictable trajectory
  • Chandigarh: -3.2pp over 7 years (0.5pp/year avg) — Already low; staying low
  • Uttarakhand, Karnataka: Consistent decline with minimal year-to-year variance
Volatile states (erratic year-to-year changes):
  • Manipur: Swings between 18% and 12% across years — Suggests fragility or data volatility
  • Goa: Minor fluctuations (±0.5pp) despite low absolute rate — Indicates noise with tiny absolute numbers
  • Mizoram: No consistent trend; actually worsened (+5.9pp) — Opposite of national direction
What volatility signals: States that jump year-to-year are either responding to temporary shocks (COVID recovery, administrative changes, measurement variations) or lack systemic improvements. Steady states are more likely to sustain progress.

The laggard paradox: Why Assam and Meghalaya are stuck

Assam and Meghalaya are unique among the high-dropout-rate states: they have remained above 15% for all seven years, even as other states moved dramatically downward. This is not just a current crisis. It is a persistent system-level problem.

Persistent above 15% (all 7 years)

  • Assam: Started 22.8% → Now 16.7% (improved 6.1pp only)
  • Meghalaya: Started 18.3% → Now 16.6% (improved 1.7pp only)

Comparison states (escaped crisis tier)

  • Arunachal Pradesh: Started 35.0% → Now 17.7% (improved 17.3pp)
  • Bihar: Started 29.5% → Now 6.8% (improved 22.7pp)

The stagnation problem

Assam and Meghalaya have not improved at the pace of any other high-baseline state. Even Arunachal Pradesh—starting at an even higher 35%—cut its rate more than half as fast as the national average. The contrast between Assam's 6.1pp reduction over 7 years versus Bihar's 22.7pp reduction points to a system-level barrier: infrastructure, teacher retention, economic opportunity, or other factors that Bihar was able to shift but these two were not.

Key insight for intervention:
  • If Bihar could improve 22.7pp, the question is not "is improvement possible?" but "why did Assam move only 6.1pp?"
  • The gap between Assam and Arunachal Pradesh (also in Northeast) suggests regional factors are not the only explanation
  • Meghalaya's 1.7pp movement is the slowest of all states — slower even than small improvements in already-low-dropout states
This data does not explain why. It only proves the asymmetry exists. Investigating that asymmetry—why Assam's system moved slowly while Bihar's moved fast—is the next research question.

The gender paradox: Boys are now at-risk

This briefing is titled "Girls' Secondary Dropout," but the data reveals a counterintuitive reality: boys now drop out MORE than girls in 35 of 36 states. This is a policy inflection point that changes the intervention strategy entirely.

The national pattern (Secondary 9-10):
  • Boys dropout: 12.7% nationally
  • Girls dropout: 9.2% nationally
  • Gap: Boys 3.5pp higher in dropout rates
  • Exception: Only Lakshadweep has girls exceeding boys

Largest gender gaps (Boys > Girls)

Chhattisgarh Boys 19.2%, Girls 11.6% (7.6pp gap)
Dadra & N.H. Boys 16.1%, Girls 8.6% (7.5pp gap)
Karnataka Boys 21.9%, Girls 14.6% (7.3pp gap)
West Bengal Boys 23.4%, Girls 16.7% (6.7pp gap)
Tamil Nadu Boys 11.6%, Girls 5.3% (6.3pp gap)

Why boys dropout more

Promotion rate: Boys 81.0% vs Girls 85.8% (Boys fail more)
Repetition rate: Boys 6.1% vs Girls 4.8% (Boys repeat more)
Academic result: More failure → more repetition → more dropout
Implication: Problem is NOT just enrollment—it's pedagogy & engagement
What this reveals about policy: Previous interventions focused on girls' enrollment (scholarships, toilets, safety). These worked—girls persist even after failing a grade. But boys, upon failing, are more likely to leave school entirely. This suggests boys need different support: academic mentoring, curriculum engagement, or labor market alternatives to make staying meaningful.
States that BUCK the trend (lowest gender gaps):
  • Arunachal Pradesh: Boys 19.0%, Girls 17.7% (Gap: 1.3pp) — Nearly gender-equal at high absolute rates
  • Jammu & Kashmir: Boys 13.6%, Girls 12.2% (Gap: 1.4pp) — Both very high, almost equal
  • Nagaland: Boys 13.0%, Girls 11.2% (Gap: 1.8pp) — Smaller gap in Northeast

What this analysis does NOT explain

This data proves boys dropout more and shows the mechanism (failure → repetition → exit). It does not explain WHY boys fail more often: Is it curriculum design? Teacher pedagogy? Parental expectations? Early employment pull? Peer culture? Answering that requires qualitative research in high-dropout states (West Bengal, Karnataka, Chhattisgarh) where the gender gap exceeds 7 percentage points.

What the data shows and does not

This briefing is facts only.

The data proves dropout rates, state-by-state trends, and the size of the gap. It does not prove why states improved or stalled. It does not identify which policy, system change, or intervention caused the movement. Claiming mechanism requires additional evidence beyond this dataset.

Safe conclusions

  • The national trend is improving (17.1% → 9.6%)
  • State divergence is real and persistent (1.1% to 17.7% in latest year)
  • The remaining problem is concentrated in five states
  • Some states improved sharply; others stayed flat
  • Mechanisms causing these patterns remain unknown from this data alone

Not safe to claim from this data

  • Which specific policy or intervention caused improvement
  • School-level performance (the data is state-level only)
  • Budget or spending levels (not present in UDISE+)
  • Teacher effectiveness or staffing impact
  • Root cause of stagnation in Meghalaya or persistent high rates in Assam
